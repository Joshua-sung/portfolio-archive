## Situation / Task

As an Army officer and outpost commander, I was assigned to handle a night flare firing exercise. The training involved loud noise and bright light, and similar exercises had previously caused residents to mistake the event for an emergency and submit complaints.

The task was to execute the training without civil complaints while keeping nearby residents and public agencies informed enough to interpret the event correctly.

## Action

I first reviewed how nearby units had handled similar training and where complaints had occurred. Then I identified relevant public agencies, including police and fire stations, and shared the training schedule and expected signals so incoming reports could be understood in context.

For local residents, I visited village leaders and explained the schedule, noise, light, and expected duration. A single notice was not enough because many residents were away during the day, so I repeated the communication three times: three weeks before, one week before, and on the day of training.

The repeated cadence helped convert a surprising event into an expected one. It also gave village leaders enough time to use local broadcasts and direct communication with residents.

## Result

The night training was completed with zero civil complaints. The upper unit also recognized that the resident response had been handled well.

## Tools & Tech

- Stakeholder mapping across residents and public agencies
- Prior-case review from nearby units
- Repeated resident briefing cadence
- Public agency coordination
- Risk communication plan

## Collaboration

The work connected military stakeholders, village leaders, residents, police, and fire stations. My role was to make sure every group understood the same facts before the event created confusion.

## Lessons

Risk management often depends on timing and repetition, not only the content of the message. When stakeholders receive practical information before they need it, execution becomes calmer and more predictable.
