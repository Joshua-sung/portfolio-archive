## Situation / Task

While working as a bartender and later bar manager in Dublin, I saw that the existing service-zone layout no longer matched the store's order mix. Beer still drove a large share of sales, but cocktail and mixed-drink orders were increasing. During peak time, the bar became congested and order handling slowed.

The task was to improve service flow so the team could handle a more diverse drink mix without adding unnecessary movement or staff confusion. This case is included because it shows the same operating pattern as PM work: diagnose a bottleneck, benchmark alternatives, change the workflow, and measure the business response.

## Action

I first gathered team feedback to identify where movement and order handling slowed down. Then, on days off, I visited ten well-known nearby pubs and compared their bar paths, tap placement, cocktail station setup, and customer-facing menu flow.

Based on the benchmarking, I reorganized beer taps by type and style so customers could compare choices faster. I also consolidated cocktail tools and base spirits into one work zone so the cocktail operator could complete orders from a single position during peak time.

The redesign treated customer movement and staff movement as one operating system. The goal was not only to make the bar look cleaner, but to reduce handoffs, crossing paths, and repeated searching during high-volume hours.

## Result

Average service time dropped from roughly 3-4 minutes to under 2 minutes and 30 seconds per order. Congestion inside the bar decreased, more orders could be processed during peak periods, and sales increased by about 8%. Google reviews also increased by 30 in one month, with positive comments around beer selection and faster service.

## Tools & Tech

- Competitor benchmarking across ten nearby pubs
- Team feedback collection
- Service flow and station mapping
- Beer tap and cocktail station redesign
- Review and sales monitoring

## Collaboration

The work required alignment with staff who had to use the new layout during real peak shifts. I used their feedback to define the bottleneck, then translated outside benchmarking into practical changes the team could adopt.

## Lessons

Operational design is not limited to software or dashboards. In a physical service environment, the same principle applies: observe the bottleneck, compare alternatives, redesign the workflow, and measure whether speed and customer response improve.
